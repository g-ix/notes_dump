What the timeline actually allows
	•	The oldest surviving writing appears ~3400–3200 BCE (Sumerian cuneiform), so we don’t have 10,000-year-old texts. Oral traditions may be older, but extant texts aren’t.  ￼
	•	The Rigveda’s oldest layers are late 2nd millennium BCE; Ramayana and Mahabharata were compiled centuries later (broadly 7th–4th c. BCE and 4th c. BCE–4th c. CE respectively).  ￼

A cross-civilizational sampler of “AI before AI”

Greece (myth + engineering)
	•	Hephaestus’s golden handmaids in the Iliad are literal “robot assistants”—they have voice, strength, and mind (νόος) and help the god in his forge. That’s a 2,700-year-old vision of embodied, intelligent helpers.  ￼
	•	Talos, the bronze guardian of Crete, is a giant automaton who patrols the island; ancient sources treat him as an artificial being.  ￼
	•	Hero of Alexandria (1st c. CE) wrote how-to manuals for automata—temple doors that open by themselves, theatrical machines, and feedback-like pneumatics. Think early “control systems” rather than minds.  ￼
	•	Antikythera mechanism (2nd–1st c. BCE): a geared analog computer for celestial cycles. It’s computation—just not neural.  ￼

China (myth + clockwork)
	•	The Liezi recounts Yan Shi presenting King Mu of Zhou a lifelike mechanical man with moving organs; the story is philosophic literature (compiled later), but it’s a canonical “android” tale.  ￼
	•	Legends of Mozi/Lu Ban describe wooden birds that fly (likely early kites/gliders)—proto-aeronautics narratives, not AGI, but clearly “artificial life” imagery.  ￼
	•	Su Song’s (11th c.) astronomical clock-tower used a chain-drive and automata figurines to “announce” time—programmable displays driven by a precise mechanism.  ￼

India (language as code, logic as inference, mythic tech)
	•	Pāṇini’s Aṣṭādhyāyī (c. 4th c. BCE) is a formal generative grammar—basically an ancient rule-based compiler for Sanskrit. Modern formal language theory owes it a debt.  ￼
	•	Nyāya and broader Indian epistemology formalized inference (anumāna), evidence sources (pramāṇas), and error theory—key ingredients of symbolic AI and reasoning engines.  ￼
	•	Samarāṅgaṇasūtradhāra (11th c.) includes a Yantra chapter with mechanical birds/automata and even a coy “I won’t reveal construction secrets” for flying machines—visionary, not a blueprint.  ￼
	•	Vaimānika Śāstra is not ancient aeronautics: a 1974 IISc study found it modern and technically unworkable. Use it as mythology, not engineering.  ￼
	•	Astras in the epics are “smart” weapons in narrative form—target-seeking, context-dependent—closer to conceptual guidance systems than literal devices.

Jewish thought (language as creation, ethics of making life)
	•	Sefer Yetzirah (3rd–6th c. CE) describes creation via letters + numbers (sefirot)—a striking information-as-cosmos frame. Later, golem legends depict life animated by permutations of letters—think “symbolic program” animating matter—and warn about control/ethics.  ￼

Egypt (mind/speech as world-builder, illusion-tech)
	•	The Memphite Theology (Shabaka Stone) says Ptah creates with heart (mind) and tongue (speech)—idea → word → reality. That’s proto-“code compiles the world.”  ￼
	•	The Colossi of Memnon “sang” at dawn due to thermal cracks—ancient acoustic automaton vibes to observers.  ￼

Islamic Golden Age (programmable machines)
	•	Banū Mūsā (9th c.) catalogued ingenious devices—valves, fountains, self-regulating mechanisms.  ￼
	•	Al-Jazarī (1206) built automata, including a programmable musical boat (change peg patterns → change performance). That’s literal pre-digital programming.  ￼

“Quantum” & mind metaphors (careful mapping)
	•	Ancient cosmological timescales (yugas/kalpas) run into billions of years—no physics, but a comfort with vast scales and cyclical time that resonates with modern cosmology’s orders of magnitude.  ￼
	•	Philosophical schools (Vedānta, Yogācāra, Daoism) probe mind-world relations, illusion, and perception—conceptual neighbors to cognitive science, not to quantum mechanics per se. (Use them as metaphors, not data.)

Patterns that rhyme with AGI
	1.	Artificial servants/guardians. Golden handmaids, Talos, golems, mechanical men—ancients imagined embodied agents with voice, skill, even “mind.”  ￼
	2.	Programmability & feedback. From Hero’s temple doors to Al-Jazarī’s peg-programmed musicians to clockwork automata—control logic long predates silicon.  ￼
	3.	Language as engine. Pāṇini’s formal rules and Sefer Yetzirah’s letter-cosmos both treat symbol manipulation as world-shaping—exactly the intuition behind compilers and, abstractly, code.  ￼
	4.	Ethics & safety. Golem tales (create → protect → risk) are early alignment parables; Greek myths (Promethean overreach, Talos) warn about control and unintended consequences.  ￼

About “dragons,” star-origins, and lost tech

Myth isn’t lab notes—but it is compressed cultural memory. Folklore about flying beings, fiery weapons, or crafted life reads like symbolic containers for power, control, and awe. There’s zero hard evidence of a prior techno-civilization behind them, and the archaeological and textual record we do have starts thousands (not tens of thousands) of years ago. Still, it’s fair to say the imagination of AGI-like agency (artificial minds, programmable bodies, language as creation) is ancient and cross-cultural.  ￼
